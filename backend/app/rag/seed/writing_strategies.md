# IELTS Writing Strategies

## Task 1 (Academic) — Report Writing

### Structure (aim for 160-190 words in 20 minutes)
1. **Introduction (1 sentence)**: paraphrase the question rubric. Never copy it — copied rubric is deducted from the word count. Change vocabulary ("shows" → "illustrates", "the proportion of" → "the percentage of") and structure ("The graph shows car ownership from 1990 to 2010" → "The line graph illustrates how car ownership changed over a twenty-year period beginning in 1990").
2. **Overview (1-2 sentences)**: the single most important paragraph. State the main trends or most striking features WITHOUT specific data. Start with "Overall," or "In general,". A missing overview typically caps Task Achievement at Band 5.
3. **Body paragraph 1 and 2**: group the data logically (e.g. rising categories vs falling categories; first half of the period vs second half; one chart vs the other) and support every claim with selected figures.

### Data selection
- Do NOT describe every number. Select: start points, end points, peaks, lows, crossovers, and the largest/smallest values.
- Compare where the task says "make comparisons where relevant" — comparison language is expected ("twice as high as", "the gap narrowed", "whereas", "in contrast").
- Approximate correctly: "just over 40%", "roughly a third", "nearly doubled".

### By chart type
- **Line graph / bar chart over time**: trend language (rose steadily, declined sharply, fluctuated, plateaued, peaked at, dipped to). Vary structure: "There was a sharp rise in X" vs "X rose sharply".
- **Static pie/bar/table**: comparative language (the largest share, considerably more than, ranked second).
- **Process diagram**: present simple passive ("the mixture is heated"), sequencing (first, subsequently, once, at the final stage), state number of stages and start/end in the overview.
- **Maps**: past/present/future tenses as dated, location language (to the north of, adjacent to, was demolished, was converted into, was replaced by).

### Common Task 1 mistakes
- No overview, or an overview buried in a body paragraph.
- Giving opinions or explanations for the data ("this is because people prefer cars") — Task 1 is purely descriptive.
- Mechanical listing of every figure with no grouping.
- Tense errors: past periods need past tense; "since 2000" needs present perfect.
- Under 150 words (penalised) or over ~200 words (wastes Task 2 time).

## Task 1 (General Training) — Letters
- Identify the required tone: formal (unknown recipient, "Dear Sir or Madam", no contractions, "Yours faithfully"), semi-formal (known but distant, "Dear Mr Smith", "Yours sincerely"), informal (friend, "Dear Anna", contractions fine, "Best wishes").
- Cover ALL three bullet points, each developed in its own paragraph.
- State the purpose in the opening line ("I am writing to enquire about / to complain about / to thank you for...").

## Task 2 — Essay Writing (aim for 260-290 words in 40 minutes)

### Question types and required structures
1. **Opinion (agree/disagree)**: "To what extent do you agree or disagree?" — state a clear position in the introduction and maintain it throughout. Either fully agree, fully disagree, or a clearly signposted partial agreement ("I largely agree, although...").
2. **Discussion**: "Discuss both views and give your own opinion." — one body paragraph per view PLUS your own opinion, which must be explicit (usually in the introduction and conclusion, and ideally woven into the body).
3. **Problem/solution (or cause/solution)**: one paragraph on problems/causes, one on solutions; each idea developed with an explanation and example.
4. **Two-part (direct questions)**: answer BOTH questions, typically one body paragraph each. Missing one question caps Task Response at Band 5.
5. **Advantages/disadvantages**: "Do the advantages outweigh the disadvantages?" requires a judgement, not just a list.

### Four-paragraph template
- **Introduction (2 sentences)**: paraphrase the topic + thesis statement directly answering the question ("This essay argues that..." / "While X has some merit, I believe Y").
- **Body paragraph 1 & 2**: one central idea each. Structure: topic sentence → explanation (why/how) → example or consequence → optional link back to the question. Depth beats breadth: two well-developed ideas outscore four undeveloped ones.
- **Conclusion (1-2 sentences)**: restate position and main reasons. No new ideas. Start "In conclusion,".

### Thesis and position
- The position must be visible in the introduction, consistent in the body, and restated in the conclusion. "Clear position throughout" is the key Band 7 Task Response phrase.
- Avoid a "hidden opinion" saved only for the conclusion in opinion essays.

### Cohesion devices (use a range, don't over-use)
- Adding: furthermore, moreover, in addition, what is more.
- Contrast: however, nevertheless, whereas, despite this, on the other hand, although.
- Cause/result: consequently, as a result, therefore, this means that, owing to.
- Examples: for instance, a case in point is, this is exemplified by.
- Referencing to avoid repetition: this trend, such measures, these individuals, the former/the latter.
- Band 7+ cohesion is often invisible: referencing and substitution ("this", "such an approach", "doing so") rather than a connective starting every sentence. Beginning every sentence with "Firstly... Secondly... Moreover..." reads as mechanical (Band 6 behaviour).

### Common Task 2 mistakes
- Not answering the exact question asked (writing a general essay on the topic).
- Overgeneralisation without support ("everyone knows that...", "all young people...") — hedge instead ("many", "tend to", "it is often argued").
- Memorised introductions/"template" phrases irrelevant to the question — examiners disregard memorised language.
- Informal register: contractions, "kids", "a lot of", rhetorical questions, addressing the reader as "you".
- Examples that are invented statistics ("a study shows 87%...") — a plausible general example is safer and equally valid.
- Under 250 words; or spending so long on Task 1 that Task 2 (worth double) is rushed.

### Time management
- Task 1: 3 min analyse/plan → 14 min write → 3 min check.
- Task 2: 5 min plan (position + 2 main ideas + example each) → 30 min write → 5 min check (articles, subject-verb agreement, plurals, spelling of topic words).
